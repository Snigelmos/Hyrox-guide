The first time we sat down with our race files from a full season of Hyrox events and tried to find the pattern in who finished in 1:18 versus 1:34, the answer was uncomfortable: it wasn't fitness. The athletes who beat their targets had race plans they'd actually rehearsed. The ones who blew up had vague goals and a trust that fitness would carry them through.

Pacing is the lever. Here's how we plan for our own races, and what we tell anyone heading into their first or fifth Hyrox.

## Why pacing dominates

Consistent submaximal pacing produces faster total times than the alternating sprint-and-rest patterns most untrained athletes default to. The pacing literature in middle-distance running is unambiguous on this: **even and slightly negative split strategies outperform positive splits at the same average power output**. The classical reference for the cost of variable pacing is [Foster et al.'s work on pacing strategy in cycling](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8201932/), and the principles transfer cleanly to Hyrox's structure of repeated work bouts on cardiac glycolytic load.

Our practical translation: if you go out 5% too hot for the first 25% of the race, you typically pay it back at 1.5x to 2x in the back half. We've watched this play out in our own data and it's the single biggest reason first-timers underperform their fitness.

## Step 1: Know your target time

Start with the goal and work backward. The single biggest mistake we see at this step is athletes whose target is 10% optimistic for their current fitness. Use our [time benchmarks](/racing-guide/times/) to ground the target in division and age group reality, and check it against your last 5 km road TT. A rough conversion: a 22-minute 5 km road runner can typically target 1:25 Open Men's Hyrox with 8 weeks of station work.

## Step 2: Allocate the time budget

A rough split for trained Open athletes:

- Running: 45 to 50% of total time
- Stations: 38 to 42% of total time
- Transitions: 8 to 12% of total time

That last number is the one most beginners get wrong. Transitions are dead time, not rest. The pacers we coach treat the [Roxzone](/racing-guide/race-day-tips/) as a sub-3-second-per-metre jog, never a walk.

## Step 3: Set run splits

The 8 runs should follow a **negative split pattern**: start conservatively, finish strong. The temptation is always to bank time early. Resist it.

A 1:20 target (Open Men) breaks down like this:

| Run | Target pace | Why |
|-----|-------------|-----|
| Run 1 | 4:40/km | Fresh, but the adrenaline is the trap |
| Run 2 | 4:35/km | Settling in |
| Run 3 | 4:35/km | Steady state |
| Run 4 | 4:35/km | Pre-burpee, stay controlled |
| Run 5 | 4:40/km | Post-burpee, legs are heavy |
| Run 6 | 4:35/km | Finding a second wind |
| Run 7 | 4:30/km | Starting to push |
| Run 8 | 4:20/km | Everything you've got |

Total running time: roughly 36:30.

A note on Run 5: it is consistently the slowest run in our race files even when athletes plan for it to be steady. The burpee broad jumps cook the lower legs in a specific way that doesn't show up in normal training. Don't fight it. Plan for the slowdown.

## Step 4: Set station targets

Use your training times, then add a **10% buffer** for race-day fatigue and queue-line uncertainty. Race day is always harder than the controlled training brick.

For an Open Men's 1:20 target, station times we plan to hit:

| Station | Target | Buffer-adjusted target |
|---------|--------|------------------------|
| SkiErg | 4:00 | 4:24 |
| Sled push | 1:45 | 1:55 |
| Sled pull | 2:15 | 2:30 |
| Burpee broad jumps | 5:30 | 6:00 |
| Row | 3:30 | 3:50 |
| Farmers carry | 1:20 | 1:30 |
| Sandbag lunges | 4:30 | 5:00 |
| Wall balls | 4:30 | 5:00 |

Your numbers will differ. Anchor them to your actual recent training, not your aspirational best.

## Step 5: Minimise transitions

Most athletes lose 8 to 15 minutes in transitions. Target benchmarks:

- Under 10 seconds from run finish to station start
- Under 10 seconds from station finish to next run start
- Jog, never walk, through every transition zone

Keep your gel pouches and salt tabs in known pockets so you don't fumble in the Roxzone. We've seen 30 second losses from a single bad pocket dive in our own race files.

## Common pacing mistakes

The four we see repeatedly:

1. **Banking time on runs 1 to 3.** Going hard early feels smart and is the most expensive mistake in the race.
2. **All-out on the SkiErg.** The first station tempts you to race it. Stay controlled. The cost of a 5-second SkiErg PR shows up at the sled push.
3. **No plan for burpees.** Station 4 needs a specific rep scheme. Most of us use 5 burpees, then a one-second stand and breath, then repeat. Pace it like a hill workout, hard but sustainable.
4. **Stopping between wall ball sets.** Breaks compound. Keep rest to 5 to 10 seconds maximum. Long breaks between sets cost more than the slightly slower pace would.

## The mental framework

We divide the race into four quarters:

1. **Q1 (stations 1 and 2).** Build rhythm. Stay under control.
2. **Q2 (stations 3 and 4).** The dark zone begins. Burpees will hurt. Dig in.
3. **Q3 (stations 5 and 6).** The grind. Maintain effort. Don't slow down.
4. **Q4 (stations 7 and 8).** The finish. Leave everything on the course.

When it gets hard, your competitors are hurting just as much. The athlete who slows down least wins.

## Common questions on race-day pacing

**What if my heart rate hits zone 5 on station 1?**
Step off the gas, immediately. Zone 5 in the first 10 minutes of a 90-minute race ends the race for you. Your peak HR window should arrive at the wall balls, not the SkiErg. The [HR zone literature on lactate threshold and time to exhaustion](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19553818/) is consistent on this.

**Do I need a heart rate monitor for pacing?**
Not strictly. We've raced great with just a paced pace plan and good listening to body. But for first-timers we recommend an HR strap (chest, not wrist, for arena interference) because HR is the single best feedback signal when adrenaline lies to you about effort. See [our Garmin Hyrox setup](/blog/garmin-hyrox-activity-profile/).

**Should the negative split apply to stations too?**
No, the opposite, actually. Station times are typically fastest in the first half because the muscles are fresh. The pacing job is to prevent the second half from being twice as slow.

**What about pacing in Doubles?**
Different game. The handover pattern dominates. We'll write that up separately, but the short version: never stand at a station if your partner is running, and never run if your partner is mid-set.
