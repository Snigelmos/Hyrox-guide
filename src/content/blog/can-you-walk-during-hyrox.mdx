## The quick answer

**Yes, you can walk anywhere on a Hyrox course.** There is no rule requiring continuous running. Athletes can walk the running sections, walk through the Roxzone, and even walk between repetitions during stations (like wall balls or burpee broad jumps).

But "you can walk" is not the same as "you should walk everywhere." Knowing **when** to walk is the difference between a race-saving pacing strategy and a costly mistake.

---

## Where walking is allowed

| Section | Walking allowed? | Strategic? |
|---------|-----------------|------------|
| 1km running laps | Yes | Sometimes |
| Roxzone transitions | Yes | Rarely (wastes time) |
| Wall balls station (between throws) | Yes | Yes (recovery) |
| SkiErg / Rower | Walking N/A (you sit/stand at machine) | — |
| Sled push (forward motion only) | Walking pace is normal | Yes |
| Sled pull | Walking backward is normal | Yes |
| Burpee broad jumps (between reps) | Yes | Sometimes |
| Sandbag lunges (between sets) | Yes | Sometimes |
| Farmers carry | Walking pace is normal | — |

So walking is permitted everywhere. The strategic question is when it serves your finish time and when it costs you.

---

## When walking is good strategy

### 1. During Run 1, if you went out too fast

If your Run 1 pace is significantly faster than your sustainable race pace, walking 100–200m to settle your heart rate is far better than trying to "tough it out" at a pace you cannot maintain.

The risk: paying for the early over-pace with a 5-minute slowdown later in the race. A 30-second walking break in Run 1 to reset your effort is one of the smartest tactical moves a first-time racer can make.

### 2. After hard stations

After the sled push, sled pull, or wall balls station, your legs and breathing need a short reset. A 30–60 second walk-jog at the start of the next 1km lap allows you to regather and then transition into running pace.

The risk: walking too long and drifting into a permanent walk that gets harder to break out of. Set yourself a clear walk distance (e.g., 100m) and commit to running again at the end of it.

### 3. Within the wall balls station

Wall balls (100 reps) is genuinely difficult. Most first-time athletes break it into 4–5 sets with brief walk-recoveries between sets. A 10-second walk-around between sets of 25 wall balls is often faster than struggling through unbroken sets and dropping the ball.

For the full wall ball strategy, see [Hyrox Wall Balls: The Make-or-Break Final Station](/blog/hyrox-wall-balls-technique/).

### 4. Within burpee broad jumps

80 burpee broad jumps is a long station. Many athletes incorporate a brief walk after every 10–15 burpees to manage their heart rate. This often results in a faster total station time than continuous reps that gradually slow to a crawl.

### 5. Within sandbag lunges

200 sandbag lunges almost always include short walk breaks where the athlete pauses between sets. Pure unbroken sandbag lunges are rare even at elite level.

## When walking is a mistake

### 1. The Roxzone

The Roxzone is the worst place to walk. You are not actively training, you are not actively recovering — you are just adding race time. Even a slow jog through the Roxzone is significantly faster than a walk, and at low intensity it does not impact your subsequent run pace.

For more, see [What is the Hyrox Roxzone?](/blog/what-is-the-hyrox-roxzone/).

### 2. The first 500m of Run 1

The first run sets your race tone. Walking from the gun (rather than starting steady and easing into running) is rarely the right call. Even slow runners should start the race at a continuous slow jog, not a walk.

The exception: athletes who have come from very limited running backgrounds and know they cannot sustain even a slow jog for 1km. For these athletes, a planned run-walk strategy from the gun is sensible.

### 3. The final 1km

Run 8 is the last 1km lap before the finish. Walking here means surrendering finish time at the moment when "just finishing" is closest. Even a fatigued athlete should muster a slow jog for the final lap.

## Run-walk strategy: a structured approach

Some athletes plan a deliberate run-walk pattern from the start, particularly first-timers without strong running backgrounds. The classic structure:

**Method 1: Distance-based.** Run 800m, walk 200m, repeat. Apply to every running lap.

**Method 2: Time-based.** Run 5 minutes, walk 1 minute, repeat. Apply to every running lap.

**Method 3: Station-based.** Run continuously when fresh, allow 100m walk recovery at the start of each lap after a hard station (sled, wall balls), then settle back into running.

Method 3 is most common among recreational Hyrox athletes who can sustain continuous running but want pre-planned recovery built in.

## Walking during the sled push

The sled push is technically a walking station even at elite level. Athletes lean into the sled and step forward — they do not run. The pace varies based on weight, athlete strength, and floor conditions, but it is universally a walk.

The strategic question is whether to attempt the entire push unbroken or to plan brief stops. Most first-time athletes plan 1–2 brief stops on the sled push. Trained athletes typically push it unbroken.

For technique, see [Hyrox Sled Push: The Station That Stops First-Timers Cold](/blog/hyrox-sled-push-technique/).

## How walking affects your overall finish time

A back-of-envelope calculation. For an 8km Hyrox running portion:

- All-running at 6:00/km: 48 minutes
- Run 7:00/km, walk 5:00/km, 80% running / 20% walking: 51 minutes
- Run 7:00/km, walk 5:00/km, 60% running / 40% walking: 53 minutes
- Run 8:00/km, walk 5:30/km, 50% running / 50% walking: 54 minutes
- Pure walking at 5:30/km: 44 minutes (for the running portion; this is unrealistic for most athletes who cannot maintain 5:30/km walking for 8km)

Walking does not destroy your finish time. A run-walk strategy can finish a Hyrox in 95–110 minutes for most athletes. But strategic walking is much faster than unstructured walking — knowing when to walk matters.
