## Why heart rate monitoring matters for Hyrox

Hyrox is a metabolic puzzle. Athletes who pace badly (typically by going out too hard on Run 1) pay for it across the rest of the race with significantly slower stations and runs. Heart rate data is the most reliable way to:

- Set proper Zone 2 pace for long runs in training
- Verify that your "easy" runs are actually easy
- Pace race day Run 1 by heart rate ceiling
- Track recovery between training sessions
- Monitor cumulative training load over weeks and months

A heart rate monitor is the single most useful piece of training tech for a Hyrox athlete after a stopwatch. This guide covers the three main categories: chest straps, watch-based optical sensors, and arm bands.

---

## The three sensor types

### 1. Chest straps (most accurate)

Chest straps measure heart rate via electrical signals from the heart muscle (ECG-style). They are the most accurate option, particularly during interval training and high-intensity efforts.

**Pros:**
- Most accurate, especially during high-intensity work
- Quick response time (1–2 seconds for changes)
- Reliable for years (typical battery life: 1+ years per coin cell)

**Cons:**
- Some athletes find them uncomfortable
- Need slight moisture to make good skin contact
- Have to remember to wear them

### 2. Optical wrist sensors (most convenient)

Modern running watches measure heart rate via LED light reflecting off blood vessels in the wrist. The technology has improved significantly in recent years.

**Pros:**
- Convenient (always on your wrist)
- No additional device to wear
- Generally accurate during steady-state running

**Cons:**
- Less accurate during high-intensity interval work
- Less accurate during station work involving wrist movement (burpees, lunges, sled pull)
- Susceptible to interference from sweat, watch position, skin tone variations

### 3. Arm bands (compromise option)

Optical sensors worn on the upper arm or forearm. A compromise between chest strap accuracy and wrist convenience.

**Pros:**
- More accurate than wrist optical
- More comfortable than chest strap for many athletes
- Good for high-intensity work

**Cons:**
- Still less accurate than chest strap during very high-intensity work
- Adds another device to charge and remember

## Optical wrist sensor accuracy: the honest take

Even the best optical wrist sensors are roughly **2–5% less accurate** than chest straps during intense interval work. The error is typically in one direction: optical sensors lag during rapid heart rate changes (e.g., the start of a sprint or the jump from running into the sled push).

For most Hyrox athletes, this 2–5% error is acceptable. Heart rate zones are not razor-precise to begin with — small differences in measurement do not meaningfully change training prescription.

For athletes who want maximum precision, particularly for VO2max interval work and threshold efforts, a chest strap remains the gold standard.

**A good compromise:** A modern GPS watch (Forerunner 265, Coros Pace 3) for daily use, with a Polar H10 chest strap for hard interval sessions and the race itself.

## What to look for in your Hyrox-specific data

If you have heart rate data, the key training and race insights are:

**1. Your max heart rate.** Establishes the basis for zones. Test by running progressively harder intervals until you cannot push higher (typical session: 4 × 4 minutes hard, with the last interval near maximum effort).

**2. Your aerobic threshold (AeT) heart rate.** This is the upper boundary of Zone 2 — typically around 75–82% of max HR for most athletes. Use this as your ceiling for easy runs.

**3. Your lactate threshold (LT) heart rate.** The upper boundary of Zone 3, beyond which lactate accumulates rapidly. Roughly 86–92% of max HR. This is your race-pace ceiling for the steady-state portions.

**4. Your race-day heart rate progression.** A well-paced Hyrox sees heart rate gradually rise from steady (Zone 3) on Run 1 to near-maximum on the final station and Run 8. Erratic spikes early indicate mispacing.

## Buying recommendations by budget

**Budget (£60–80):** Polar H10 chest strap. Pair with your phone or any compatible watch you already own. Accurate, durable, reliable.

**Mid-range (£200–280):** Coros Pace 3 watch with optical HR. Genuine all-in-one for training and racing. The £230 price gets you full GPS, optical HR, training metrics, and 38 hours of battery.

**Premium (£400–500):** Garmin Forerunner 265 or Coros Apex 2, plus a Polar H10 chest strap for high-precision sessions and race day. The combination covers everything.

**Hyrox-specific recommendation:** Coros Pace 3 + a £60 Polar H10 chest strap. Total: ~£300. Excellent accuracy, full features, sensible budget.

Chest straps: Specialty running retailers (Wiggle, Sportsshoes.com) and Amazon all stock at similar prices.
