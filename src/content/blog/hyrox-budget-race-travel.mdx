You found a race, the entry fee is paid, and then you check hotel prices in the host city for that weekend. For a lot of athletes that is the moment a "£100 race" quietly becomes a £400 weekend. Accommodation, not entry, is usually the single biggest cost of travelling to a Hyrox, and it is also the cost you have the most control over.

This guide compares the realistic ways to sleep cheaply around a race, what each one actually costs, and the trade-offs that matter when you have to perform the next morning.

## The options at a glance

Prices are rough per-night ranges for a race-weekend in a typical European or UK host city. Expect the top of each range in big cities (London, Berlin) and on dates close to the event.

| Option | Rough cost/night | Best for | Main trade-off |
|--------|------------------|----------|----------------|
| Hotel near venue | £90–250 | Convenience, sleeping well | Most expensive; books out early |
| Budget chain hotel | £60–120 | Predictable comfort | Often a transit ride from the venue |
| Hostel (private room) | £40–80 | Solo or pair on a tight budget | Noise, shared facilities |
| Shared Airbnb (split 3–4 ways) | £25–50 per person | Groups travelling together | Needs people to split with |
| Car or rooftop-tent camping | £0–35 | Driveable domestic races | Sleep quality, parking, showers |

The right answer depends mostly on two things: whether you are driving or flying, and how many people you are travelling with.

## Hotels: pay for sleep when it counts

The case for a hotel near the venue is simple. You sleep in a quiet, warm, dark room, you can walk or take a short ride to the start, and you are not solving logistics problems the morning of a hard race. For a goal race where you want a personal best, that is often money well spent.

To keep the cost sane:

- Book early. Host-city hotels near a Hyrox tend to fill and rise in price as the date approaches.
- Look one transit stop out. A budget chain a 10–15 minute train ride from the venue is frequently half the price of the "official" nearby hotels.
- Split a twin or family room with training partners. Two or three athletes in one room drops the per-person cost dramatically.

## Hostels: cheap, with caveats

A private room in a hostel can be a good middle ground for solo athletes or pairs, often in the £40–80 range. The risk is sleep: shared bathrooms, thin walls, and weekend crowds are not ideal the night before a race. If you go this route, pay the small premium for a private room rather than a dorm, bring earplugs and an eye mask, and check recent reviews specifically for noise.

## Shared Airbnb: best value for groups

If you are travelling with a club or a few training partners, a whole apartment split three or four ways is usually the cheapest comfortable option, often £25–50 each. You also get a kitchen, which makes race-morning breakfast and the night-before meal far easier to control. The catch is that you need the group; for a solo athlete an entire flat rarely beats a budget hotel.

## Car and rooftop-tent camping: cheapest for driveable races

If the race is within driving distance, sleeping in your vehicle is the cheapest option there is, frequently £0 to £35 a night depending on whether you use a paid campsite or a free overnight spot. It also removes the check-in and checkout timing problems and keeps your kit in one place.

It only makes sense in specific situations. You need a driveable domestic race, somewhere legal and safe to park overnight, and a setup that actually lets you sleep. A cold, cramped night in a car seat is a bad way to prepare for the sled push. If you are going to do this properly, it is worth setting the vehicle up for warmth and a flat sleeping surface rather than improvising; this [guide to budget vehicle and rooftop-tent camping setups](https://vehiclecampingguide.com/) covers the rooftop tents, sleeping platforms and insulation that make car-sleeping genuinely restful instead of something you endure.

Two practical race-specific points:

- Showers and warm-up: plan where you will shower and warm up. Many campsites have facilities; for a free overnight spot, a cheap day pass at a gym or pool near the venue solves both.
- Parking near the venue: sleeping in a quiet spot is one thing, but check you can also park near the start on race morning, or that there is easy transit from where you stayed.

## What actually matters the night before a race

Whatever you pick, judge it against race performance, not just price. The things that move the needle:

- **Sleep:** quiet, dark and warm enough to sleep through the night. This is the whole reason to spend anything at all.
- **Breakfast control:** somewhere you can eat your normal pre-race breakfast at the right time. A kitchen or a kettle and your own supplies beats hoping a cafe is open early.
- **Distance and transit:** a short, predictable trip to the venue. Race mornings are stressful enough without a 45-minute journey with bags and nerves.
- **Warmth and recovery:** somewhere to get warm and off your feet before and after.

A cheaper option that wrecks your sleep can cost you more in race-day performance than the money it saved. Spend where it protects the race, save everywhere else.

## A simple way to decide

- Flying internationally for a goal race: book a budget hotel a stop or two from the venue, or split an Airbnb with others. Do not gamble on sleep.
- Driving to a domestic race, on a tight budget: car or rooftop-tent camping, with a planned shower and a parking check.
- Travelling as a group: shared Airbnb almost always wins on cost and convenience.

## Common questions

Is it a bad idea to sleep in my car before a race? Not if you set it up to sleep well. A flat, insulated, warm sleeping space and a plan for a morning shower can be perfectly fine for a driveable race. An unplanned night folded into a car seat is what hurts performance, not the car itself.

How early should I book accommodation? As early as you commit to the race. Host-city prices typically climb as the date nears and the closest, best-value places sell out first. Booking early is the cheapest single thing you can do.

What is the cheapest option overall? For a group, a shared Airbnb split several ways. For a solo athlete driving to a domestic race, car or rooftop-tent camping. For a solo athlete flying in, a budget hotel a short transit ride from the venue usually wins on the balance of cost and sleep.
