## The honest summary

Compression products fall into three useful categories for Hyrox:

**1. Calf sleeves** — Modest benefit during running and races, more clear benefit for recovery. **Worth considering** if you have calf cramping issues or are racing in cold weather.

**2. Knee sleeves** — Helpful for athletes with knee issues during heavy squatting (sandbag lunges, wall balls). **Worth considering** if you have a history of knee pain.

**3. Full compression tights and tops** — Mostly aesthetic and warmth-related, with minimal performance benefit during a race. **Optional** based on personal preference.

Outside these three, most compression products marketed for Hyrox do not provide meaningful benefits beyond what good base-layer running gear already offers.

## Calf sleeves: when they help

### What calf sleeves actually do

Quality compression calf sleeves apply graduated pressure (tighter at the ankle, looser at the knee). This:

- Improves blood return to the heart
- May reduce muscle vibration during running
- Provides modest warmth to the calf muscle
- Some evidence suggests reduced delayed onset muscle soreness (DOMS) post-exercise

### When calf sleeves help in Hyrox

**During the race:** Modest benefit. The 8 km of running is the relevant context — sleeves can reduce calf fatigue late in the race for some athletes. Athletes prone to calf cramps or tightness often report meaningful improvement.

**Post-race recovery:** Clearer benefit. Wearing sleeves for 4–6 hours after the race appears to reduce soreness for the following days, helping you return to training faster.

**In cold conditions:** Useful for warmth. Outdoor Hyrox events in winter or cold venues benefit from the additional calf insulation.

### Sizing matters

Calf sleeves are sized by calf circumference, not shoe size. Measure the widest part of your calf and follow the brand's sizing chart. A loose sleeve provides no compression benefit; an overly tight sleeve cuts off circulation. The middle of the brand's recommended range is typically correct.

## Knee sleeves: a separate category

### What knee sleeves do

Knee sleeves provide warmth, modest support, and proprioceptive feedback (you become more aware of knee position). They do not significantly increase strength or prevent serious injury. They are not the same as a knee brace.

### When knee sleeves help in Hyrox

**Athletes with previous knee issues:** Sleeves can reduce discomfort during deep squat patterns (sandbag lunges, wall balls) by providing warmth and slight compression.

**Cold conditions:** Cold knees feel stiffer. Warmth from sleeves can help maintain mobility.

**Heavy lifters transitioning to Hyrox:** Athletes used to knee sleeves in the gym for squatting often want them for the wall balls and lunges stations.

### Top knee sleeve options for Hyrox

For Hyrox specifically, you want **lightweight running-style sleeves** rather than heavy powerlifting sleeves. The latter are too thick for high-rep work.

## Full compression tights: real benefit or marketing?

Full-leg compression tights claim:
- Improved running economy (limited evidence)
- Reduced muscle fatigue (some evidence)
- Faster recovery (clearer evidence post-exercise)
- "Recovery" benefit during sleep (limited evidence)

In a Hyrox context, full compression tights provide modest benefit during the race itself. The clearer benefit is post-race wear (as recovery).

If you are buying compression tights primarily for the race:
- Consider whether the warmth is appropriate for your venue
- Check that they are designed for athletic movement (some "compression" tights are actually shapewear and bind during deep squats)
- Verify the pricing — premium compression tights can cost £80–120, which is hard to justify for a small race-day benefit

---

## Compression tops and arm sleeves

Compression tops and arm sleeves provide minimal benefit during a Hyrox. The upper-body demand is dominated by the sled pull, rowing, and SkiErg — these stations stress arm and back muscles in patterns that compression sleeves do not meaningfully aid.

If you wear compression tops in training and prefer the feel, race in them. If you do not currently wear them, do not purchase them specifically for Hyrox.

## What about graduated socks?

Graduated compression socks (full sock with graduated compression from ankle to upper calf) combine the calf sleeve benefit with the additional foot/ankle compression. Some athletes prefer these to separate sleeves.

| Compression sock | Notable |
|------------------|---------|
| CEP Run Tall Compression Socks | Premium reference |
| 2XU Vectr Compression Socks | Lightweight, breathable |
| Compressport Pro Racing Socks v3 | Lightweight, racing-focused |
| Bauerfeind Run Compression Socks | Premium, durable |

Costs are similar to calf sleeves (£20–50) but socks add the foot coverage. Choice between sleeves and socks is largely personal preference — the compression benefit is similar.

## Things compression products do not do

To set realistic expectations, compression products do **not**:
- Increase your strength
- Replace proper warm-up
- Prevent injury (they may reduce some risk but do not prevent injuries from technique errors or excessive load)
- Significantly improve running economy at recreational paces
- Replace recovery (sleep, nutrition) post-exercise

If you are looking for a single piece of gear that will significantly drop your finish time, compression products are not it. They are marginal gains.

## Buying guidance

**Budget-conscious approach:**
- One pair of quality calf sleeves (£25–35) — useful for race day and recovery
- Skip the rest unless specific needs arise

**Mid-range approach:**
- Calf sleeves + one pair of compression knee sleeves (£60–100 total)
- Full compression tights only if you also use them for training

**Premium approach:**
- Calf sleeves + knee sleeves + compression tights + recovery tights for post-exercise (£200–350 total)

The diminishing returns on compression products are significant. A single pair of quality calf sleeves provides 80% of the practical benefit available in this category.
