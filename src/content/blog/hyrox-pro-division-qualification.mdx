## The structure of Hyrox competition

Hyrox operates three competition tiers within the same race:

**Open:** The general category. Any athlete can enter. No qualifying required. Roughly 90% of Hyrox competitors race in Open.

**Pro:** The competitive elite category. Athletes must qualify to enter. Pro athletes race in a separate wave, typically at the start of the event, and compete for prize money at select events.

**Elite 15:** The top 15 finishers in each Pro category at the Hyrox World Championship. The pinnacle of recreational Hyrox competition.

Understanding which tier you are targeting — and what each requires — is essential before structuring your training and racing calendar.

---

## How to qualify for Pro

There are three routes to the Pro category:

### Route 1: Hit the qualifying time standard in Open

The most straightforward route. Race an Open event and finish under the qualifying time for your age and gender category. The exact standards update each season and can be found on hyrox.com.

**Approximate qualifying standards for Pro (as of 2025–26 season):**
- Open Men (General): approximately sub-57–60 min (varies by age group)
- Open Women (General): approximately sub-68–72 min
- Masters categories have adjusted standards

These are approximate — check the current season's standards on the official Hyrox website as they are updated annually.

### Route 2: Win or podium your age group

At qualifying events, top finishers in each age group earn automatic Pro qualification regardless of absolute time. This route is most relevant for masters athletes (40+, 50+, 60+) where age-group fields are smaller.

### Route 3: Season ranking points

Hyrox runs a global ranking system where athletes accumulate points from their top performances across the season. Athletes who accumulate sufficient ranking points qualify for the World Championship in their category.

## What Pro racing is actually like

### The start wave

Pro athletes race in a separate start wave at the beginning of the event, before the Open waves. This means a less crowded course (particularly relevant for the sled tracks) and more visible competition.

### The competitive environment

Pro fields are genuinely elite. The average Pro finisher is typically 5–10 minutes faster than the Open-to-Pro qualifier threshold. At the top of the Pro category are athletes training 12–20 hours per week with decades of competitive background.

Do not expect to be competitive in your first Pro race unless you are significantly under the qualifying standard. Qualifying for Pro is the achievement — being in the mix takes additional time.

### Prize money

Prize money is available at select Hyrox events (typically major city events) and at the World Championship. For recreational athletes, prize money is not a realistic expectation unless you are finishing in the top 3–5 of a Pro category.

## The gap between Open sub-60 and Pro performance

Sub-60 in Open is a significant achievement — it places you in the top 3–5% of Open competitors. But the step from sub-60 to genuine Pro performance (sub-55 for men, sub-65 for women) is substantial.

Consider what sub-55 requires versus sub-60:

| Section | Sub-60 target | Sub-55 target |
|---------|--------------|--------------|
| Each run (average) | ~4:10/km | ~3:45/km |
| SkiErg | ~3:25 | ~3:05 |
| Sled Push | ~1:15 | ~1:05 |
| Wall Balls | ~4:15 | ~3:45 |

The additional 5 minutes across a 60-minute race represents approximately 8% improvement in overall output. At this level, that requires a significant increase in VO2max, running speed, and station-specific power.

Most athletes who qualify for Pro are at the "qualified but not yet competitive" stage for 1–2 seasons before their training catches up to the field.

## Is it realistic for an amateur to train to Pro standard?

Honestly: for a minority of motivated recreational athletes with the right physical profile, yes.

**The profile most likely to reach Pro:**
- Strong aerobic background (previous competitive running, cycling, or similar)
- Able to commit 10–14 hours/week to training
- 2–4 year development timeline from beginning Hyrox to Pro qualification
- Strong coachability and training discipline

**The profile that will likely remain competitive Open:**
- Training limited to 6–8 hours/week
- Starting from a low fitness base
- Does not have the aerobic history that takes years to build

There is nothing wrong with being a competitive Open athlete. The Open category is where the majority of the Hyrox community races, and being in the top 5–10% of Open is a genuine athletic achievement.

---

## Training for Pro: what changes

Athletes seriously targeting Pro qualification need:

**Higher running volume:** 50–60 km/week minimum to develop the running fitness for Pro-level splits. This requires systematic progressive overload over 1–2 years.

**Station-specific power:** Pro-level sled push times require genuine lower body power beyond what most recreational strength training develops. Heavy squats and trap bar deadlifts become non-optional.

**Year-round structured programming:** Pro qualification requires consistent training across multiple seasons. The athletes who qualify typically have 2–3 years of progressive Hyrox-specific training behind them.

**Racing experience:** Knowing how to race at Pro pace — which is above Zone 4 for most of the event — requires practising at that intensity repeatedly in training and racing.
