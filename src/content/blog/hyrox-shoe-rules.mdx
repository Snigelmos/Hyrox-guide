**Hyrox has no official shoe rule.** Any closed-toe athletic shoe is legal. The unwritten constraints are:

1. **No spikes or cleats** that damage the venue floor.
2. **No outdoor shoes** caked with mud or grit (clean shoes only).
3. **No bare feet, sandals, or open-toed footwear.**

That's it. Beyond those obvious limits, you can race in any shoe.

The harder question is "what *should* you wear", and the answer depends on what you're optimising for.

## The only formal restriction

Hyrox's rulebook simply requires:

> Athletes must wear closed-toe athletic footwear suitable for indoor sport. Footwear must not damage the venue surface.

Spikes, golf cleats, and football studs are the practical no-go list. Everything else is your call.

## What's banned in practice

- **Track spikes** (any spike under the forefoot)
- **Football / rugby boots with studs**
- **Any metal-cleated shoe**
- **Open-toed shoes, sandals, hiking boots, work boots**
- **Visibly muddy outdoor shoes** (judges may turn you away)

## What's allowed but suboptimal

- **Carbon-plated marathon shoes** (Nike Vaporfly, Adidas Adios Pro, etc.), fast on running, but unstable on the sled push and risky on the lunges. Not banned, just risky.
- **Pure max-cushion running shoes** (Hoka Bondi, Brooks Glycerin Max), soft underfoot, slow energy return on sled push.
- **Old-school CrossFit shoes** (early Nike Romaleos), lifting-shoe heel raises hurt running.

## What top finishers actually wear

Three categories dominate the Hyrox podium:

### 1. Hybrid trainers (most popular for serious athletes)

Designed specifically for the Hyrox-style mix of running + functional work:

- **Puma Fast-R Hyrox** (official Hyrox sponsor)
- **Puma Deviate Nitro Hyrox**
- **Nike Pegasus** with cross-training tweaks
- **On Cloudflyer**
- **TYR CXT-2**

These shoes prioritise a stable platform under load, decent cushioning for running, and grip for the sled push.

### 2. Lightweight road runners (fastest on running)

For athletes whose finish time is run-limited:

- **Saucony Endorphin Speed**
- **Asics Magic Speed**
- **Adidas Boston**
- **Nike Vomero / Pegasus**

Best if you're under ~80 kg, have strong ankles, and your sled push isn't a weak point.

### 3. CrossFit-style training shoes (most versatile, slight running penalty)

For first-timers and stations-dominant athletes:

- **Nike Metcon 9**
- **NoBull Trainer / Trainer+**
- **Reebok Nano X4**
- **TYR CXT-1**

Adds 30-90 seconds to your finish vs a runner, but you're never wishing you had more grip on the sled push.

## Can you change shoes mid-race?

Technically yes, see [our full guide on shoe changes](/blog/can-you-change-shoes-during-hyrox/). Practically: it costs 30-60 seconds and is rarely worth it.

## Race-day shoe checklist

1. **Race in shoes you've trained in for at least 6 weeks.**
2. **Don't race in brand-new shoes:** even a perfect model out of the box will give you blisters by km 5.
3. **Bring a backup pair** in your bag in case of last-minute issues (broken lace, etc.).
4. **Lace them snugly:** looser than running lacing, tighter than walking. Especially across the bridge of the foot for the sled push.
5. **Tape any hot spots** before race day if you have known blister-prone areas.

## What about shoe cleaning?

Some venues (e.g. London ExCeL, Tempelhof Berlin) have started informally checking that outdoor shoes have been cleaned before entering the race floor. Bring a cloth and wipe down the soles before warm-up. This isn't an official rule, but a venue staff member can refuse you if your shoes are visibly muddy.
