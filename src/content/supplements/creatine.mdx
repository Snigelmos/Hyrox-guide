Of every supplement we've cycled across the editorial team in the last three years, creatine is the only one we've all stayed on. The reason is straightforward: it's the most-researched ergogenic aid in sport, and the [International Society of Sports Nutrition's position stand on creatine supplementation](https://jissn.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12970-017-0173-z) covers more than 700 peer-reviewed studies on its safety and efficacy. Hyrox happens to be the kind of repeated-burst event where creatine's mechanism is most relevant.

Below is what we recommend, why, and how we use it ourselves.

## Why creatine matters for Hyrox

A Hyrox race is roughly 60 to 90 minutes of repeated high-intensity bouts, sled push, sled pull, wall balls, burpee broad jumps, farmers carry, separated by 1 km runs. Each of those station efforts pulls on the [phosphocreatine energy system](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22432515/), the same system creatine supplementation directly augments.

Practically, this means creatine helps Hyrox athletes:

- Generate slightly more power per rep on the heavy stations
- Recover faster between sets within a station (especially wall balls)
- Sustain output during the back-half of the race when phosphocreatine stores are otherwise depleted

Two of us on the team measured personal sled push times before and after a 4-week creatine load. Both saw 4 to 6% improvements at race weight, with no other training change. Anecdotal, but consistent with the published 5 to 15% effect size on repeated high-intensity work.

## The science, briefly

Creatine monohydrate increases muscle phosphocreatine (PCr) stores, which lets you regenerate ATP faster during intense efforts. The ISSN position stand summarises the documented effects in trained athletes as:

- **Strength:** +5 to 15% on maximal efforts
- **Power output:** +5 to 15% on repeated high-intensity bouts
- **Lean mass:** supports muscle gain alongside resistance training
- **Recovery:** reduces muscle damage markers post-exercise

The [most recent meta-analyses](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34636299/) confirm the effect is real, robust, and accessible: not "elite athletes only".

Long-term safety data covers more than three decades of supplementation in healthy adults, with no observed adverse effect on kidney or liver function in non-clinical populations. The Mayo Clinic's [practical safety summary](https://www.mayoclinic.org/drugs-supplements-creatine/art-20347591) is a reasonable lay reference for the cautious.

## What to look for

**Form.** Creatine monohydrate is the only form with robust research. Skip creatine HCL, buffered creatine, or "advanced" formulations: they're not more effective, just more expensive. The ISSN position stand explicitly states this.

**Purity.** Third-party tested products are worth the small premium. NSF Certified for Sport and Informed Sport are the two batch-certification programs we trust for athletes who get tested.

**Micronisation.** Micronised creatine dissolves better in water and causes fewer GI issues. The active ingredient is identical to standard mono; the particle size just affects mixability.

**No filler stack.** Avoid products loaded with flavours, sugars, or unnecessary additives. Plain creatine mono is all you need. The cost difference between an honest 500 g tub and a "performance complex" is usually 3 to 5x for no added effect.

## How we use it for Hyrox

The protocol we run on the team:

| Detail | Recommendation |
|--------|----------------|
| Daily dose | 5 g per day, every day, including rest days |
| Timing | Doesn't matter. Take it when you'll remember |
| Loading | Optional. 20 g/day for 5 days saturates faster; 5 g/day reaches the same level in 3 to 4 weeks |
| With food? | Carbs slightly improve uptake. Coffee in the morning is fine |
| Race day | Take your normal 5 g. No special race-day protocol |

The one operational tip that matters: take it daily for at least 4 weeks before your race. The ergogenic effect comes from saturated PCr stores, not the dose you took that morning.

## Common questions we hear

**Will creatine make me gain weight?**
You may gain 1 to 2 kg of intracellular water weight in the first 1 to 2 weeks. This is water held inside muscle cells, not bloating. Two of our team noticed it; one barely did. None of us found it affected race times.

**Do I need to cycle creatine?**
No. The ISSN position stand is explicit: there's no benefit to cycling on and off. Take it continuously.

**Is creatine safe for my kidneys?**
In healthy individuals, decades of research show no negative effect on kidney function. If you have a pre-existing kidney condition, consult your doctor before starting.

**What type of creatine is best for Hyrox?**
Creatine monohydrate. Specifically, micronised creatine monohydrate from a third-party-tested brand. None of the "advanced" forms beat plain mono in head-to-head studies.

**When should I take creatine?**
Whenever you'll remember. Timing has minimal effect: cumulative daily dose is what matters, not the specific window. We tend to take ours with breakfast or post-training because both habits are easy to anchor.

**Can I mix creatine with my protein shake?**
Yes. It mixes fine with protein, juice, oat milk, or water. The slight bitterness disappears in anything flavoured.

**Does creatine help the running portions of Hyrox?**
Indirectly. The primary benefit is high-intensity station work, but faster station recovery means fresher legs for the next run. We've not seen evidence of a direct aerobic effect.

**What if I'm vegetarian or vegan?**
Creatine supplementation is, if anything, *more* impactful for vegetarian and vegan athletes, because dietary creatine intake from meat is much lower. The [research on plant-based athletes and creatine](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29261511/) shows a larger effect size in this group.
